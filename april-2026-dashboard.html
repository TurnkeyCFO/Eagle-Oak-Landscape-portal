CFO commentary

What matters most coming out of April

The operating story is better than the company profile alone would suggest, but the next layer of improvement depends on pricing discipline, route design, and faster billing-to-cash conversion.

1. Recurring maintenance is doing exactly what it should

At 60% of revenue, recurring work is stabilizing labor absorption and giving the business a base from which to add higher-margin projects. That reduces volatility and makes growth more controllable than a project-only model.

2. Productivity improved, but Eagle Oak is not yet at best-in-class crew economics

April's utilization and labor percentage are respectable, yet revenue per employee still trails stronger operators. The next win is route density and minimum pricing discipline on small accounts, not broad cost-cutting.

3. Payroll remains the real cash stress test

Even in a good month, weekly payroll hits before all invoices are collected. A tighter A/R cadence, cleaner deposits on install work, and faster completion billing will do more for financial resilience than trimming office overhead.

4. Pricing power is likely hiding inside account mix

The profile suggests too many small or underpriced contracts. If management prunes low-density stops and pushes enhancement upsells through the best property managers, EBITDA can expand without adding much headcount.

Modeled recommendations for May: raise minimum stop pricing on low-density routes, move irrigation repairs to same-week billing, review the bottom 10 accounts by gross profit per visit, and tie crew scorecards to billable hours, callbacks, and route completion time.
